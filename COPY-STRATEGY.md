# Cookies & Tokens — Website Copy Strategy & Rewrite

A full audit and rewrite of the current one page site, written against the copy that is actually live today (quoted throughout), not a hypothetical version.

House voice rules honored in all proposed copy: no em dashes, no hyphens in prose, none of the standard agency filler, concrete commercial nouns over slogans.

---

## 1. Executive diagnosis

The current site is well above the agency baseline. It is not AI slop. It has a genuinely ownable metaphor (the purple cookie as human taste, the teal token as AI), a real point of view ("real experience runs the AI, not the other way around"), and several lines that actually land ("Premium craft at the speed of 2019", "Scale without soul", "Great people. Missing a gear."). The motion and structure already signal taste and budget. Whoever wrote this has an ear.

The problem is not quality of writing. The problem is that the site sells the studio's **operating model** more than it sells the **buyer's outcome**, and it gives a skeptical prospect with money almost nothing to underwrite the decision.

Four gaps cost the most conversions:

1. **No named audience.** The copy talks to "most companies", "teams that want all of it", and "you". A serious buyer cannot tell in three seconds whether this studio is for a seed stage founder, a Series B marketing lead, an owner operated retailer, or an enterprise. The site never says who it is for, so everyone has to self qualify, and most will not bother.

2. **No proof, and the proof gap is unmanaged.** Being early is fine. Being early and silent about why that is safe is not. The site currently has zero credibility anchors a buyer can lean on: no founder track record, no logos, no numbers, no named work, no risk reversal. The honesty is admirable, but honesty without credibility reads as "unproven", not "trustworthy".

3. **The model is described more than the result.** Large stretches explain how the work gets made (the cookie, the token, "every piece is reviewed by a person", "AI in the production pipeline"). That is reassurance, not a reason to buy. The buyer's question is "what will this do for my business", and the answer arrives mostly as process.

4. **The offering is incomplete and slightly duplicated.** Growth is missing experiential and activations entirely, which is a live revenue lever for exactly the launch driven brands this studio should target. Separately, the "What We Do" signpost section and the three full service sections cover the same ground twice, which dilutes momentum.

The metaphor is the asset. The fix is to keep the metaphor and the voice, point them harder at the buyer's outcome, add experiential as a first class growth engine, and give a careful buyer a credible reason to act before the single CTA at the very bottom.

### Quoted lines that underperform

- **"Most companies run a creative agency, a performance shop, and a dev contractor who never speak to each other."** Good insight, but it describes the buyer's *vendors*, not the buyer. Who is "most companies"? A two person startup does not run three vendors.
- **"We compress that, so nothing gets lost in translation, because there is no translation."** The double back is clever but makes the reader work. It also restates the previous sentence.
- **"You get the output of a team three times the size, made by people who put their name on every piece."** Strong line, but it appears in spirit three separate times (Collision, TokenSide, About). Repetition drains its punch, and "three times the size" is an input claim, not an outcome.
- **"For teams that want all of it."** This is the closest the site comes to naming an audience, and it names no one. "All of it" is not a segment.
- **"We are early, and we would rather show you real engagements than invented ones."** Honest and likable, but it leads with the weakness and gives the buyer nothing credible to hold instead.
- **"Book 30 minutes"** Generic, calendar first, and asks for time before the buyer has a reason to give it.
- **"Growth across every channel that pays."** Strong headline, but the section then omits the one channel built for launches and attention (experiential), which undercuts "every".

## 3. Strategic positioning options

Template: *We help [specific client] achieve [specific outcome] by combining [distinct capabilities] without [common frustration or tradeoff].*

**Option A — Safe / clear**
> We help founders and small marketing teams get a brand, a growth engine, and a product that move as one, by combining creative, marketing, and software in a single senior team, without managing three vendors who never talk.

**Option B — Strong / differentiated (recommended)**
> We help funded and founder led brands turn launches into compounding demand, by combining senior brand, growth, experiential, and engineering talent with AI in the pipeline, without the agency bloat, the handoffs, or the wait measured in quarters.

**Option C — Bold / premium**
> We help category challengers create real world attention that converts online, by combining brand, growth, activations, and software under one roof at three times the usual pace, without junior work, vendor seams, or a single line we would not put our name on.

**Recommendation:** lead with **B**. It names a buyer (funded and founder led brands), states a commercial outcome (compounding demand from launches), folds experiential into the capability stack so the new offering feels native, and keeps the studio's own language ("put our name on", "three times"). Borrow C's phrase **"real world attention that converts online"** as the connective tissue for the experiential story, because it states the through line in six words: the real world creates attention, the system converts it.

## 4. Recommended messaging strategy

Page job: take a referred, skeptical, budget holding buyer from "what is this" to "these people get the whole system, and I would not be taking a risk" in one scroll.

### Hero
- **Job:** make the buyer understand the category and the bet in five seconds, and feel they are in expensive, capable hands.
- **Think / feel:** "One team for brand, growth, and product, run by senior people. That is rare and it would save me the vendor circus."
- **Mistake to avoid:** leading with the AI mechanic. AI is the engine, not the promise. Do not let "human plus AI" beat "brand, growth, and product that ship together".
- **Proof here:** a single credibility line under the sub (where the founders have worked, at the category level) plus the implicit proof of the craft and motion itself.

### Supporting subhead
- **Job:** convert the headline into a concrete buyer outcome and name the pain.
- **Think / feel:** "That is exactly my problem, and they fix the specific thing that slows me down."
- **Mistake to avoid:** restating the headline. The sub must add the *who* and the *what you get*, not echo "one studio".
- **Proof here:** speed and seniority claims that are true and falsifiable ("most of it in weeks", "senior team, no juniors on your account").

### Primary CTA
- **Job:** capture the ready buyer with an operator framed, low ego ask.
- **Think / feel:** "I can just describe what I am building and get a real person, not a sales funnel."
- **Mistake to avoid:** calendar first language. Ask for the problem, not the meeting.
- **Proof here:** response promise ("a founder replies, usually same day").

### Secondary CTA
- **Job:** catch the not yet ready buyer with a diagnostic that gives value before the call.
- **Think / feel:** "I can get a useful read without committing to a pitch."
- **Mistake to avoid:** making it another "book a call" in different words.
- **Proof here:** what they walk away with ("a short teardown of your brand, funnel, or stack").

### Proof strategy
- **Job:** give a careful buyer something to underwrite the decision, without inventing clients.
- **Think / feel:** "These are senior operators with real track records, and the risk of working with a young studio is managed."
- **Mistake to avoid:** faking logos or numbers, or apologizing for being early. Reframe early as access and seniority.
- **Proof here, in priority order:** founder track record at the category level, named work the moment it exists `[bracketed placeholders until then]`, a guarantee or risk reversal, the "senior only, no juniors" promise, and method transparency.

### Services architecture
- **Job:** show one coherent system (brand, demand, product) rather than a menu.
- **Think / feel:** "They can build the brand, drive the demand including in the real world, and ship the product, and those three reinforce each other."
- **Mistake to avoid:** listing services as disconnected line items, and burying experiential.
- **Proof here:** the demand loop made explicit (activation creates content, content feeds paid and social, audience feeds CRM and retargeting).

### Process / methodology
- **Job:** de risk the engagement by showing a sane, outcome first way of working.
- **Think / feel:** "They define success before they build, and a human owns the bar."
- **Mistake to avoid:** describing the method as the value. The method reassures, the outcome sells.
- **Proof here:** the "define what good means before anything ships" promise, plus a concrete first build timeframe.

### Case study framing
- **Job:** prove outcomes with one real, named engagement and one real result.
- **Think / feel:** "They did a specific thing for a specific client and it worked."
- **Mistake to avoid:** a wall of logos with no story, or staying empty when a real result exists.
- **Proof here:** Client, the problem, what was built across brand, growth, and product, and one number.

### FAQ / objection handling
- **Job:** answer the five questions that stop a funded buyer from replying.
- **Think / feel:** "They have already addressed my hesitation, so I do not have to raise it."
- **Mistake to avoid:** soft, generic FAQs ("what is your process"). Use the real objections: proven, cost, AI means generic, young studio risk, single service.
- **Proof here:** the guarantee, the seniority, the pricing posture, and the AI quality control.

### Final CTA
- **Job:** close with the same operator tone and remove the last friction.
- **Think / feel:** "One message, one real conversation, no deck theater."
- **Mistake to avoid:** introducing a new idea here. Closing time is for the ask, not new arguments.
- **Proof here:** response promise repeated, plus the human reassurance line.

## 5. Recommended services architecture

One system, three pillars, with growth wired together so experiential is native rather than bolted on.

### Pillar 1 — Brand (the cookie, purple)
- **Problem it solves:** the brand is forgettable, inconsistent across channels, or easy to copy, so marketing spend works harder than it should.
- **Deliverables:** brand strategy and positioning, naming, visual identity and design system, messaging, content and copy systems, art direction, campaign concepts, production at volume.
- **Outcomes clients care about:** recognition, premium pricing power, lower cost per acquisition because the brand does some of the selling, consistency at scale.
- **Best for:** brands pre launch, mid rebrand, or scaling content past what their current setup can hold a bar on.

### Pillar 2 — Marketing / Growth (the engine)
- **Problem it solves:** channels are run as disconnected line items by people who do not talk, so spend leaks and nothing compounds.
- **Sub services:**
  - **Campaign strategy** — the plan that decides what to say, where, and in what order.
  - **Performance / paid media** — paid social and search that scale profitably, with creative testing built in.
  - **Lifecycle / CRM / email** — list building and automated flows that turn audiences into repeat revenue.
  - **Content / social** — the always on output that keeps the brand in feed.
  - **Organic / community / partnerships** — the compounding channels and relationships most agencies skip.
  - **PR / earned media** — stories worth covering and the outreach to land them.
  - **SEO / discoverability** — being found when intent is high (folded into organic and search where relevant).
  - **Experiential / activations** — the real world layer. See below.
- **Outcomes clients care about:** pipeline, CAC and ROAS, retention and LTV, share of attention, demand that grows without proportional spend.
- **Best for:** brands with a product worth talking about and a number they need to move.

#### Experiential / Activations — positioned as an engine, not an event service
Launches, pop ups, field activations, brand experiences, and event linked campaigns. The point is not the party. The point is the loop:

> **The real world creates attention. The system converts it.**

- An **activation** creates the moment, the crowd, and a reason to care.
- That moment is engineered to **capture content** (the studio shoots it for reuse, not for a recap).
- The content **feeds social and paid**, so the reach outlives the day.
- The audience in the room **feeds the list and the retargeting pool**, so attention becomes addressable demand.
- **Earned media and community** amplify it, and the brand recall accelerates conversion downstream.
- Everything is **measured**: attendance, content produced, list growth, assisted conversions, and the lift in branded search and direct traffic after the moment.

This is what makes experiential coherent with the rest of the offering. It is a top of funnel demand and content engine that hands off cleanly to paid, lifecycle, and product, run by the same team that builds the brand and the funnel, so nothing is lost between the moment and the money.

- **Outcomes clients care about:** launch momentum, brand salience, a library of authentic content, list and community growth, and a measurable lift in demand after the event.
- **Best for:** consumer and prosumer brands with a launch, a flagship moment, a new market, or a community to build.

### Pillar 3 — Build / Product / Software (the token, teal)
- **Problem it solves:** off the shelf does not fit, internal work eats the team's hours, or the product needs to ship and the dev shop is slow and disconnected from the brand.
- **Deliverables:** marketing sites, web apps and dashboards, native and cross platform mobile apps, custom internal tools and integrations, full SaaS products, and AI agents that run support, ops, research, and reporting.
- **Outcomes clients care about:** time to ship, hours saved, a product that matches the brand, software that scales without a rebuild.
- **Best for:** brands that need a real product or tool, not just a campaign, and want it to share a soul with the marketing.

**Why one roof:** the brand informs the growth, the growth informs the product, and the activation feeds all three. Three vendors cannot do that, because the value is in the seams they create and this studio removes.

## 7. Ten conversion improvements

1. **Name the buyer in the hero or the band right under it.** Replace "most companies" and "teams that want all of it" with the real segment (funded and founder led brands, consumer and prosumer brands with a launch). Self qualification kills conversion.
2. **Add a CTA above the fold and at least one mid scroll.** Right now the only ask is after a long scroll. Add the operator CTA in the hero and a soft one after the growth section.
3. **Introduce a diagnostic secondary CTA.** "Get a free teardown" gives value before the call and captures the not yet ready buyer who will never "book 30 minutes".
4. **Add a proof band under the hero.** Founder track record, senior only promise, response time. Turn "early" into "you get the founders".
5. **Add an FAQ that answers the five real objections.** Proven, cost, AI generic, young studio risk, single service. Each answered objection is a recovered exit.
6. **State outcomes as numbers, even framed ones.** "First build live in [N] days", "[X] day turnaround", and real results the moment they exist. Adjectives do not underwrite a decision.
7. **Add a risk reversal.** A small guarantee (a paid pilot, a defined first milestone, a money back first sprint, or "we agree the definition of done before we start") lowers the cost of saying yes to a young studio.
8. **Cut the duplication between the signpost and the three service sections.** Make the signpost a fast index that links down, not a second full description, so momentum builds instead of repeating.
9. **Make experiential visibly part of growth, not an afterthought.** The new card plus the demand loop in the section intro signals "these people understand the whole system", which is the exact impression the brief wants.
10. **Add a response promise to both CTAs.** "A founder replies, usually same day" removes the fear of shouting into a void, which is the silent objection on every contact form.

3. **Logo strip** of clients and partners, even one or two to start. A single real logo outperforms a paragraph of claims.
4. **A guarantee or risk reversal** (paid pilot, defined first milestone, first sprint money back, or "definition of done agreed up front").
5. **A concrete speed proof**, such as a real first build timeframe or a turnaround range, stated as a number.
6. **A short founder note or face**, even a single line and a photo, so the buyer knows who they are hiring.
7. **One outcome metric** from any channel (a launch's reach, a list's growth, a campaign's ROAS), shown as a stat the moment it exists.
8. **A testimonial or referral quote**, one sentence from a real person, including from prior in house or agency roles if client work is not ready.
9. **Process artifacts**, such as a sample teardown or a one page scope, to prove the diagnostic CTA delivers something real.
10. **Press, awards, or category credibility** if any exist, including talks, communities, or notable prior work the founders shipped.

## 10. CTA options (better than "Book a call")

**Strategy led**
1. Map your brand, growth, and product in one call.
2. Pressure test your growth plan with us.

**Operator led**
3. Tell us what you're building.
4. Send us the problem, get a real reply.

**Premium**
5. Start a conversation, not a pitch.
6. Work with the founders, not a funnel.

**Approachable**
7. Say hello, no deck required.
8. Tell us where you're stuck.

**Diagnostic / audit style**
9. Get a free teardown of your brand, funnel, or stack.
10. Book a 20 minute fit call.
